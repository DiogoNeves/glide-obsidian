# Purchase Research

Use when the user asks for a thing to buy, a replacement product, or a good deal.

## Defaults

- Start by clarifying the failure mode or real job to be done.
- Do not treat store ranking or star rating as enough evidence.
- For food, health, child, electrical, safety, or high-cost items, bias toward reputable brands, official sellers, safer materials, and support over marginal savings.

## Evidence

1. Check at least one tested editorial, professional, or domain-expert review source when available.
2. Check review volume, rating distribution, and recent negative reviews, especially durability, safety, counterfeit, leakage, battery, heat, coating, or support failures.
3. Prefer listings with a stable brand history, clear specs, and a seller identity that matches the brand or a trusted retailer.
4. Treat suspiciously high ratings, vague reviews, variant-swapped listings, low review counts, unknown brands, and extreme discounts as risk signals.

## Price

1. Use price-history or comparison tools where possible.
2. Judge price against recent normal price, not crossed-out retail price.
3. For commodity items, check whether a known reliable model costs only slightly more than an unknown-brand bargain.
4. Recommend a buy-now option only when quality evidence and current price both look good; otherwise suggest a watch price or shortlist.

## Output

- Clear top pick.
- Budget or alternative pick when useful.
- Avoid criteria.
- Main tradeoff: price, durability, safety, convenience, size, aesthetics, or support.
- Sources and live-data limitations.
